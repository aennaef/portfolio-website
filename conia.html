From client project to product launch

Bytabo© initially collaborated with a medium-sized metal construction company to develop the idea of an employee app.
After successfully implementing the project, recognizing common issues in mid-sized companies with high shares of non-desk workers,
and noticing a gap in the market,it was decided to launch the app as its own product.
I joined the team just before this pivotal decision, stepping into the role of the product designer.

The process

Due to pre-existing design and development of key app features, the process for implementation in conia couldn't be fully mapped out in the beginnig.
Upon receiving the rebranded app, I took the following steps:

  1. Understanding the product
  2. Expanding the brand’s visual language
  3. Improving old designs based on customer feedback
  4. Sketching out user flows & wireframes for new features

Understanding the product

Before I started designing, I aimed to understand the product, the users and typical use cases as thoroughly as possible.
I therefore started by learning about the background of product development.

Background

Middle-sized companies often struggle with ineffective employee communication practices, resulting in fragmented information flows,
collaboration between teams, and a disconnect between management and staff, ultimately leading to high fluctuation rates and decreased productivity.

The problem

Companies need a quick and easy way to share important information with their non-desk employees and simplify complicated,
labor-intensive tasks to boost employee motivation and satisfaction and ultimately ensure the productivity of the company.

The solution

A native mobile app, available for iOS and Android, that can be installed on employees' smartphones and allows company managers
to send relevant information to their employees, ensuring they receive important information in a timely manner.

Original home screen from customer project.

Expanding the app’s visual language

My first objective after obtaining the newly branded app was to expand the brand's visual language to ensure consistency and appeal.
To accomplish this, I began creating onboarding screens that not only introduced users to the app's functions, but also developed a consistent visual brand. brI experimented with several visual directions, and in total, 36 distinct screens were created.

Preference test

To determine the visual direction, I conducted a preference test between my two favourite sets (n = 27).
I found that nearly two-thirds of the participants preferred the variant with gradient bubbles. Based on this result, I proceeded confidently with this direction.

Onboarding screens

Note: Due to limited resources at this time, it was ultimately decided not to implement onboarding walkthrough screens (3-6), but they may be implemented in the future.

Design improvements

In just a few months, we acquired four customers and began receiving app store reviews.
Some customers even contacted our product owner with requests for improvements. Building on the foundation laid by previous iterations,
I analysed user feedback to identify areas for improvement. By carefully reviewing user insights and pain points,
I iteratively refined the existing designs to enhance usability and address user needs.

Missing hierarchy on profile page

  • Users don’t know how to log out of the app because they cannot find the logout button.
  • Users don’t understand CTA ”Änderungen beantragen”
    (Engl.“request change” (Users have to press this button if they would like to request profile changes regarding their position, department etc.). It’s barely needed but was the most prominent CTA.

The changes

  • Introduced tab menus for switching between profile and account settings
  • Renamed button into “request profile changes”
  • Added "App settings" section, allowing users to adjust language preferences and toggle notifications
  • Easy to detect, unambigous logout button

Lack of customization and difficulties scrolling through long lists

  • A number of customers had problems with distinguishing the correct departments
  • Some customers expressed a desire for more customization options within the app

The changes

  • Photos for locations make it easier to recognize them
  • Customazible photos for single departments make the experience more personalized

New features

Conia began as an MVP, but our customers quickly expressed needs for more features to make corporate communication even easier.
Key requests included: document sharing for schedules and HR files, a feedback feature, and a chat function.
I initiated the process by crafting user flows and user stories and then iteratively designing wireframes for each feature.

Document feature

As a production worker who doesn't work near the HR department, I would like to have my payslips sent to me by app so that I no longer have to pick them up in the office during my break.

Feedback feature

As a production employee without access to a company email address or the phone numbers of those in charge memorized,
I need a way to provide feedback to my employer to ensure my suggestions are heard.

Chat

As a team lead in production, I need a quick and easy way to distribute information to all team members so I can save time and not have to communicate it to each person individually.

Documents

Sharing documents via conia streamlines access to essential information, fostering transparency, enabling quick updates, and empowering employees,
ultimately enhancing productivity, efficiency, and organizational alignment.

Feedback

The feedback feature with the option to upload photos empowers users to directly voice concerns or report broken infrastructure to their employer
benabling organizational improvements, and enhancing workplace satisfaction and productivity.

Chat

The chat feature enables instant messaging, group chats, and file sharing among colleagues, fostering fast and efficient communication,
collaboration, and problem-solving but also a way for employees to organize non-work related interests together.

My takeaways

Adhering to an existent design scheme

One of the new challenges for me was working with an already established interface by following its existing design scheme.
Whenever I was developing a new feature or wanted to improve the existing design by changing UI elements, I had to search for existing patterns before creating a new element.
Initially, this was a difficult task, but with some practice I found ways to reuse patterns while also improving the user experience (e.g. when redesigning the profile section with a tab menu).

Balancing functionality and simplicity

Balancing a comprehensive feature set with a clutter-free user experience is challenging within financial and technical constraints.
While the initial product had only five different features placed on the home screen, it’s now possible to have placed up to ten features with similar functions on the home screen
(e.g. News articles can be posted in the news feature and files can be posted in the document feature with the content also being “news content”). This is not ideal but due to budget limits restructuring of the app’s features and navigation was not possible so far.

The challenges of product development in an agency

Allocating time and resources to product development in the midst of a busy agency schedule with multiple client projects was not always possible,
as different team members were involved in multiple projects simultaneously, resulting in delays and some features (feedback & chat) not being developed (yet).
