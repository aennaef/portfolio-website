From client project to product launch

Bytabo© initially collaborated with a medium-sized metal construction company to develop the idea of an employee app. After successfully implementing the project, recognizing common issues in mid-sized companies with high shares of non-desk workers, and noticing a gap in the market,it was decided to launch the app as its own product. I joined the team just before this pivotal decision, stepping into the role of the product designer.

The process

Due to pre-existing design and development of key app features, the process for implementation in conia couldn't be fully mapped out in the beginnig. Upon receiving the rebranded app, I took the following steps:

  1. Understanding the product
  2. Expanding the brand’s visual language
  3. Improving old designs based on customer feedback
  4. Sketching out user flows & wireframes for new features

Background

Middle-sized companies in the production industries often struggle with ineffective employee communication practices, resulting in fragmented information flows, poor collaboration between teams, and a disconnect between management and staff, ultimately leading to high fluctuation rates and decreased productivity.

The problem

Companies need a quick and easy way to share important information with their non-desk employees and simplify complicated, labor-intensive tasks to boost employee motivation and satisfaction and ultimately ensure the productivity of the company.

The solution

A native mobile app, available for iOS and Android, that can be installed on employees' smartphones and allows company managers to send relevant information to their employees, ensuring they receive important information in a timely manner.

Home screen from customer project.

Expanding the app’s visual language

My first objective after obtaining the newly branded app was to expand the brand's visual language to ensure consistency and appeal. To accomplish this, I began creating onboarding screens that not only introduced users to the app's functions, but also developed a consistent visual brand. I experimented with several visual directions, and in total, 36 distinct screens were created.

Preference test

To determine the visual direction, I conducted a preference test between my two favourite sets (n = 27). I found that nearly two-thirds of the participants preferred the variant with gradient bubbles. Based on this result, I proceeded confidently with this direction.

Onboarding screens

Note: Due to limited resources at this time, it was ultimately decided not to implement onboarding walkthrough screens (3-6), but they may be implemented in the future.

Design improvements

In just a few months, we acquired four customers and began receiving app store reviews. Some customers even contacted our product owner with requests for improvements. Building on the foundation laid by previous iterations, I analysed user feedback to identify areas for improvement. By carefully reviewing user insights and pain points, I iteratively refined the existing designs to enhance usability and address user needs.

Missing hierarchy on profile page

In the original design, users couldn't find the logout button and didn't understand the "Request Changes" CTA for profile updates. To fix this, I added tab menus for profile and account settings, renamed the button to "Request Profile Changes," included an "App Settings" section for language preferences and notifications, and made the logout button prominent and clear.

Lack of customization

A number of customers were having trouble differentiating between long lists of locations or departments and were missing a more personalized interface. To address this issue, we added the option to upload photos to make list items more recognizable and provide a more personalized experience.

New features

Conia began as an MVP, but our customers quickly expressed needs for more features to make corporate communication even easier. Key requests included: document sharing for schedules and HR files, a feedback feature, and a chat function. I initiated the process by crafting user flows and user stories and then iteratively designing wireframes for each feature.

Document feature

As a production worker who doesn't work near the HR department, I would like to have my payslips sent to me by app so that I no longer have to pick them up in the office during my break.

Feedback feature

As a production employee without access to a company email address or the phone numbers of those in charge memorized, I need a way to provide feedback to my employer to ensure my suggestions are heard.

Chat

As a team lead in production, I need a quick and easy way to distribute information to all team members so I can save time and not have to communicate it to each person individually.

My takeaways

Adhering to an existent design scheme

One of the new challenges for me was working with an already established interface by following its existing design scheme. Whenever I was developing a new feature or wanted to improve the existing design by changing UI elements, I had to search for existing patterns before creating a new element. Initially, this was a difficult task, but with some practice I found ways to reuse patterns while also improving the user experience (e.g. when redesigning the profile section with a tab menu).

Balancing functionality and simplicity

Balancing a comprehensive feature set with a clutter-free user experience is challenging within financial and technical constraints. While the initial product had only five different features placed on the home screen, it’s now possible to have placed up to ten features with similar functions on the home screen (e.g. News articles can be posted in the news feature and files can be posted in the document feature with the content also being “news content”). This is not ideal but due to budget limits restructuring of the app’s features and navigation was not possible so far.

The challenges of product development in an agency

Allocating time and resources to product development in the midst of a busy agency schedule with multiple client projects was not always possible, as different team members were involved in multiple projects simultaneously, resulting in delays and some features (feedback & chat) not being developed (yet).
