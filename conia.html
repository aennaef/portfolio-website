From client project to product launch

Bytabo© initially collaborated with a medium-sized metal construction company to develop the idea of an employee app.
After successfully implementing the project, recognizing common issues in mid-sized companies with high shares of non-desk workers,
and noticing a gap in the market, it was decided to launch the app as its own product.
I joined the team just before this pivotal decision, stepping into the role of the product designer.

The process

Due to pre-existing design and development of key app features, the process for implementation in conia couldn't be fully mapped out in the beginning.
Upon receiving the rebranded app, I took the following steps:

  1. Understanding the product
  2. Expanding the brand’s visual language
  3. Improving old designs based on customer feedback
  4. Sketching out user flows & wireframes for new features

Understanding the product

Before I started designing, I aimed to understand the product, the users and typical use cases as thoroughly as possible.
I therefore started by learning about the background of product development.

Background

Middle-sized companies often struggle with ineffective employee communication practices, resulting in fragmented information flows,
collaboration between teams, and a disconnect between management and staff, ultimately leading to high fluctuation rates and decreased productivity.

The problem

Companies need a quick and easy way to share important information with their non-desk employees and simplify complicated,
labor-intensive tasks to boost employee motivation and satisfaction and ultimately ensure the productivity of the company.

The solution

A native mobile app, available for iOS and Android, that can be installed on employees' smartphones and allows company managers
to send relevant information to their employees, ensuring they receive important information in a timely manner.

Original home screen from customer project.

Expanding the app’s visual language

My first objective after obtaining the newly branded app was to expand the brand's visual language to ensure consistency and appeal.
To accomplish this, I began creating onboarding screens that not only introduced users to the app's functions, but also developed a consistent visual brand.
I experimented with several visual directions, and in total, 36 distinct screens were created.

Preference test

To determine the visual direction, I conducted a preference test between my two favorite sets (n = 27).
I found that nearly two-thirds of the participants preferred the variant with gradient bubbles. Based on this result, I proceeded confidently with this direction.

Improving old designs

Having chosen the visual direction, I proceeded to update older designs and expand them to reflect customer feedback.
One example was the home screen of the app: while the customer preferred a concise home screen with relevant widgets, their users found it
overwhelming and confusing. Based on this feedback, I developed an updated home screen design that focuses on a clean and intuitive user experience,
offering a clear overview of essential information without clutter.
